ABOUT ME


Hi y'all!

My name is James Weichert (pronouns: he/him) and I am a sophomore from Virginia studying computer science, data science, and public policy. I am running to be the next ASUC Academic Affairs Vice President (AAVP).

I'm running for AAVP because I believe that I can hit the ground running at the start of my term and be a tireless advocate for students on academic matters. I'm running because my knowledge and experiences on matters of academic policy, student fees, technology infrastructure, and ASUC administration would be uniquely useful in the Office of Academic Affairs* and would benefit students across this campus.

My campaign is focused on showing students that the next AAVP needs to be both knowledgeable of academic policies and campus bureaucratic procedures and be able to be a consistent voice for students, every day and in every meeting. To that end, I'm campaigning based off of my experiences and visions for the Office, and not based on party politics or trying to pad my resume. If there is one reason why you vote for me, it should be because I have the policies and embody the vision necessary to move this campus in a better direction. With a potential return to in-person classes this fall, it is pivotal that the next AAVP be able to seize this critical moment to push vigorously for a fundamentally different approach to academics on campus, an approach that centers students and uplifts equity and support for all students.

Read more about my experience and platforms below.




EXPERIENCE


AAVP Head of Staff*

I am the current Head of Staff* to ASUC Academic Affairs Vice President* Nicole Anyanwu. In that role, I lead the Office of Academic Affairs* and coordinate advocacy and outreach across all of the office's departments. As a senior leader in the office, I work with the AAVP* and other senior staff to formulate the office's policy stances and academic projects. Additionally, I accompany AAVP* Anyanwu in meetings with senior campus leaders, providing unique insights and advocating for student needs when it comes to academic policies. In fall 2020, I co-led the office's efforts to implement optional P/NP for major requirements for the 2020-2021 academic year. After working tirelessly throughout the semester, we were able to give Berkeley students much-needed academic flexibility in the form of P/NP for all undergraduate colleges.

Finally, I represent the Office of Academic Affairs* and the ASUC* at large on a variety of campus administrative and Academic Senate committees, including:

  • Academic Senate Committee on Admissions, Enrollment, and Preparatory Education*
  • Campus Committee on Campus Policy and Management*
  • Research, Teaching, and Learning Executive Advisory Group*
  • Campus Academic Program Committee on the Center for Connected Learning* (Moffitt Library Renovation)
  • Campus Academic Program Committee on the Data Hub Project*
  • Campus Academic Program Committee on the Hesse-O'Brien Renovation*
  • Campus Academic Program Committee on the North Field Academic Building* (Evans Temporary Replacement)

Berkeley Student Fee Referenda Committee (SFRC) Co-Chair*

Aside from my work in the ASUC*, I am the co-chair of the campus' Student Fee Referenda Committee* (SFRC), having been appointed to the role by the Chancellor's Advisory Committee on Student Services and Fees* (CACSSF) in spring 2020. SFRC* is responsible for overseeing the process by which mandatory campus-wide student fees are drafted and voted on by the student body. The committee provides oversight and guidance regarding compliance with campus and University policies and recommends improvements in campus referenda policies to the Chancellor*. As a co-chair, I lead and manage the committee and provide input on referenda practices as a member of CACSSF*.

In leading SFRC* over the past year, I have prioritized the responsible and student-centered management and governance of the entire Berkeley student fee ecosystem. Working with administrators, student leaders, and fee proponents, I have facilitated discussions around how to promote a better understanding of what student fee dollars are being used for and to require continual student-led oversight of the efficacy and need of the more than $40 million dollars in annual student fee revenue. Sitting as a non-voting member of CACSSF* has also provided me with insight into the Student Services Fee (SSF) grant process, which is a crucial source of funding for many student initiatives. Above all, I have worked to safeguard the right to student self-governance on campus-based student fees, a central part of the student experience and ethos at Berkeley.


ASUC Elections Council Chair* (2019-2020)

During the 2019-2020 academic year, I was the ASUC Elections Council Chair* and oversaw the ASUC elections process. I was responsible for organizing and executing the 2020 ASUC election, which saw a student turnout of 11,100 students, an increase from the previous year. In my capacity as Elections Council Chair*, I was charged with navigating a complex and opaque ASUC bureaucracy and for effectively communicating various bylaws to candidates and the general student body. I used my position in the ASUC* to push for transformational changes to the Association's elections process in favor of transparency, equity, and accessibility. I wrote and passed a number of ASUC Senate* resolutions relating to elections processes, including a resolution that established a temporary ad-hoc committee to study the feasibility of public campaign financing for ASUC elections. The committee continued even after the end of my term on the Elections Council* and produced a report highlighting the enormous impact on financial accessibility that public campaign funding would have on this campus.


Berkeley Student Technology Council*

As a member of the campus' Student Technology Council*, I provide senior campus IT leadership* with insight about student needs and perspectives on campus technology infrastructure, including feedback on new technology initiatives.




POLICIES


Let's Have an Honest Conversation About "Academic Accommodations"


As this year's fight for P/NP and other academic accommodations has shown, it is crucial that the Academic Affairs Vice President be capable of presenting and lobbying for a robust set of student-centered academic policies. The Office of the AAVP* encompasses a broad range of responsibilities, from academic policies to leadership on campus budget and student fee priorities to appointing ASUC representatives to campus committees. As such, the AAVP needs to have broad knowledge of how this campus operates and an overarching vision for how to lead this campus forward. Below are the nine platforms I am running on and why they matter.

1

Zoom University

Implement a responsible and effective transition to in-person instruction and promote academic policy accommodations for remote and hybrid learning.

  • Work to extend necessary academic accommodations (late change deadlines, P/NP, grading leniency) for the entirety of remote learning.
  • Prioritize most in-need student groups for in-person instruction (e.g. international students, students with disabilities) before full return to campus.
  • Work closely with campus leaders to facilitate an organized transition back to in-person instruction for the whole campus for fall 2021.
    • Provide input on plans for modifying instructional spaces and resources to facilitate reduced capacity and social distancing where appropriate.
  • Increase the availability of important courses/labs when campus returns to in-person instruction.
  • Explore possibilities for providing an online-option for some courses and academic resources even beyond the pandemic.
    • Improve course capture infrastructure in lecture halls and classrooms to allow for fully hybrid instruction through recorded lectures.
  • Advocate for continued flexibility for students unable to return to campus in the fall, including international students or immunocompromised students.

2

Post-Pandemic Academics

Solidify equitable academic policies for all Cal students. Leverage the 'blank slate' of a return to in-person instruction to demand large-scale reforms to academic policies and practices.

  • Reimagine capped major admissions to ease the pressure on students to meet arbitrary GPA cutoffs. Provide opportunities and support for all students to succeed in the major of their choice.
    • Tackle the issue of resource strain among capped majors by lobbying the campus to fully fund in-demand majors, offer more courses, and increase instructional support.
  • Provide more options, flexibility, and leniency for students facing extenuating/extraordinary circumstances during a semester.
    • Re-instate the retroactive drop option in the College of Letters and Science.
    • Suspend Cancel for Non-Payment indefinitely.
  • Seize this important, transitional moment to push vigorously for fundamental changes to inequitable policies.
    • Shift from higher-stakes exams to more frequent lower-stakes exams.
    • Reduce final exam time slots from 3 to 2 hours to reduce the number of exam conflicts.
    • Work towards the elimination of the Haas curve.

3

A Direct Connection

Connect students from all across campus directly to campus decision-makers.

  • Appoint experienced, diverse student leaders and representatives to administrative and Academic Senate committees.
  • Select committee appointees based on subject knowledge and diversity of experiences.
  • Work with student leaders outside of the ASUC to bring new perspectives and underrepresented voices to key committees and decision-making spaces.
  • Centralize and coordinate the reporting and dissemination of information from committees to better inform students of relevant campus updates, ensure consistent messaging, and uplift student demands.
  • Continue the work of the Office of Academic Affairs* in expanding robust student representation across a variety of key committees.
  • Emphasize the representation of Black students, Latinx students, students of color, Indigenous students, queer and trans students, first-generation/low-income students, transfers, and re-entry/veterans/non-traditional students across all committees. Provide students with direct access to committees concerning admissions, enrollment, resource allocation, and financial aid.

4

Honest Conversations

Bring students, faculty, and staff together to tackle large-scale academic culture and climate questions. Foster opportunities for open dialogue and work towards practical solutions to the campus' most pressing academic problems.

  • Facilitate campus-wide conversations among students, faculty, and administrators regarding academic climate and culture at Cal.
  • Lay the groundwork for a fundamentally new approach to:
    • Academic dishonesty and proctoring,
    • Grade deflation and class curves,
    • GPA cutoffs.
  • Push the campus to become a higher education leader in equity and student support in academic policies. Draw on student perspectives, similar policies at other institutions, and research on higher education policy to argue for large-scale policy reforms.

5

Your Money, Your Decisions

Promote the responsible and student-centered management of the Berkeley student fee ecosystem.

  • Provide experienced leadership in the oversight of student fees on the Chancellor's Advisory Committee on Student Services and Fees* (CACSSF).
  • Encourage the deliberate, organized management and coordination of student fees, both in creation and in continual oversight.
  • Place more emphasis on the entire Berkeley student fee "ecosystem" to better balance fee amounts and student needs across multiple years.
  • Promote more transparent and open access to Student Services Fee (SSF) grants via CACSSF.
  • Urge CACSSF to implement a moratorium on automatic/stepped increases to campus-based student fees above the level of inflation.

6

21st Century Campus

Revitalize and upgrade campus technology and facilities.

  • Re-envision what a 21st century campus looks like, centralizing a student-friendly perspective in the design of new campus buildings and resources. Prioritize undergraduate-accessible study spaces in new buildings.
  • Work with Berkeley IT leadership* to fund major upgrades to campus WiFi infrastructure.
  • Maximize collaboration among campus technology partners, including Student Affairs IT*, the Student Technology Fund*, the ASUC, and student groups across campus.
  • Advocate for campus technology infrastructure to be fully funded by the campus, recognizing the importance of technology in the academic experience.
  • Transition the iClicker and laptop loan programs to the Library and Student Tech Services*.

7

Welcome, Class of 20XX

Encourage the reform of Berkeley admissions practices to promote fair and equitable decision-making.

  • Coordinate advocacy across UC campuses and with the student regents* to further fairness and consistency in admissions processes and standards.
  • Advocate for UC Berkeley to emerge as a leader in holistic admissions by committing to go completely test-free with respect to the SAT, ACT, and other standardized testing options. Push back against attempts to implement a UC-specific standardized test, which will only reinforce existing inequities and compound stress on applicants.
  • Work with students and administrators to implement the new L&S CS "pre-approval" process in a way that promotes discovery of CS as a discipline and uplifts academic success within the context of high school opportunities and resources, recognizing that screening for prior CS experience skews against applicants without CS classes in high school.
  • Implement more accountability in Berkeley admissions without straying from our mission to evaluate each and every applicant in the context of their achievements, opportunities, and environment.

8

An ASUC That Works (For YOU)

Push for increased accountability and accessibility in ASUC spaces and do away with petty partisan politics.

  • Work across offices and parties on common-sense initiatives that benefit students and maximize the influence of student voices on campus.
  • Promote closer collaboration and coordination on campus budget priorities by formalizing an ASUC budget task force and incorporating budget asks in the Advocacy Agenda.
  • Work closely with the Graduate Assembly* (GA) on academic issues concerning both undergraduates and graduates, recognizing that the GA is the representative of graduate students on campus.
  • Conserve student fee dollars whenever and wherever possible by transitioning successful student-funded projects to campus units with other revenue streams.
    • Utilize savings to push for stipends for more staff members in the Office of Academic Affairs* and elsewhere, increasing the financial accessibility of doing good work in the Association.

9

Nine Campuses, One University

Formalize academic affairs connections across UC campuses.

  • Make permanent the UC-wide Academic Affairs working group (Council of AAVPs), forming it into a UC Students Association* (UCSA) ad-hoc committee.
  • Start coordinated academic advocacy efforts in front of the Regents and systemwide Academic Senate, in coordination with UCSA*, campus E(A)VPs, and the student regents*.
  • Provide input on student appointments to systemwide Academic Senate committees in coordination with other campus student associations and UCSA*.
  • Facilitate coordinated responses across campuses to major systemwide academic affairs developments, including admissions standards, transfer articulations, and systemwide Academic Senate policies.



WHY ME?


I am running for Academic Affairs Vice President because I firmly believe that I am the right person to lead the Office of Academic Affairs* and the ASUC at large, building on major victories achieved by the Office* during this academic year in support of equity and student support in academic life at Berkeley. As the current Head of Staff* for the Office of Academic Affairs*, I am best positioned to leverage my existing knowledge and relationships to hit the ground running during this pivotal time in this campus' history. I have the experience to work with administrators and faculty on day one and to achieve major victories for students even before the start of the fall semester (yes, I will work through the summer). The next Academic Affairs Vice President needs to be equipped to dive head-first into the academic problems that have not only plagued students during the pandemic, but also before the outset of remote instruction. I am the right person for the job.

But the ASUC Academic Affairs Vice President cannot be solely focused on just academic policy. The portfolio of responsibilities of the AAVP involves appointing all ASUC representatives to administrative and Academic Senate* committees, providing input on student fee management through CACSSF* and the Wellness Fund*, coordinating with other ASUC leaders in the development of campus technology infrastructure, promoting student wellness and professional development, and being a leader in the Association and on campus. In this respect, I will also draw heavily on my experiences as co-chair* of the campus Student Fee Referenda Committee* (SFRC), member of the Student Technology Council*, and former ASUC Elections Council Chair*. I hope to combine my existing familiarity with ASUC administrative processes and bylaws, knowledge of the Berkeley student fee ecosystem, and passion for technology to augment every aspect of my work as AAVP. Now, more than ever, this campus needs an Academic Affairs Vice President that can work towards solutions in all facets of campus student life. As AAVP, I would bring together my hands-on experience in the Office of Academic Affairs*, knowledge of the student fee system and concern for student-led oversight, and my leadership on campus and in the Association to fully support students on this campus. I am running for Academic Affairs Vice President because I want to help you succeed at Berkeley.
